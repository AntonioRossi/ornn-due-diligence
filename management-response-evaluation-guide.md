# Ornn Management Response Evaluation Guide

As of **April 6, 2026**.

## Purpose

This document converts the main open diligence questions into a substantive underwriting rubric. It is meant to help an investor or diligence lead separate polished management rhetoric from operating reality.

The prior version overweighted whether management could "explain" the business. This version is stricter. A coherent answer without documents, data, controls, or external corroboration is not enough.

## How to score each question

Each question should be scored on **two tracks**:

- **Management representation**: Did management answer directly, precisely, and consistently?
- **Proof status**: Do documents, transactional data, policies, system evidence, and third-party corroboration support the answer?

**Rule:** the overall rating for a question defaults to the weaker of the two tracks.

- A polished answer with no support cannot score above **Concerning**.
- A strong document package cannot rescue a contradictory or evasive management answer.
- If management cannot explain a core operating fact, treat that as a higher-severity issue than a missing but remediable workstream.

## Failure types

When a question scores poorly, classify the failure before making an investment decision:

- **Competence / credibility failure**: management does not understand, cannot explain, or materially misstates a core fact.
- **Structural thesis failure**: the business may be real, but the economics, market structure, or defensibility do not support the investment case.
- **Remediable build gap**: the issue is real but can likely be fixed with time, budget, and counsel.
- **Proof gap**: the answer may be true, but the diligence package does not yet prove it.

## 1. What is live today, and where is the transaction evidence?

**What must be shown**

- A product-by-product matrix by entity, jurisdiction, customer type, and legal basis.
- For every product described as `live`: first transaction date, most recent transaction date, and anonymized counterparty type.
- Clear separation between `live`, `beta`, `partner-listed`, `index-only`, and `pending`.

**Acceptable**

- Management gives a clean matrix and backs each `live` product with dated activity evidence.
- The answer is consistent across management, legal docs, public disclosures, and partner announcements.
- Management is explicit if no Ornn-operated exchange is live today.

**Concerning**

- The matrix is directionally coherent, but activity proof is incomplete or delayed to follow-up.
- "Live" is used loosely for products that are thinly used, partner-dependent, or still experimental.
- The legal basis is understandable only after follow-up with counsel.

**Unacceptable**

- Management cannot say with precision which entity offers which product.
- A product is called `live` without proof of actual transactions.
- The answer changes depending on whether the question is commercial, legal, or regulatory.

**Likely failure type if poor**

- Usually `competence / credibility failure`.

## 2. Are they monitoring the de minimis threshold, or managing around it?

**What must be shown**

- Current and trailing-12-month gross notional by product and entity.
- Affiliate aggregation analysis.
- Named owner, review cadence, escalation triggers, and breach plan.
- Evidence that growth, routing, or entity structure is not being manipulated to stay under the threshold.

**Acceptable**

- Management provides exact threshold utilization and can reconcile it to internal reporting.
- It can explain how aggregation works and who signs off on the analysis.
- It addresses the incentive problem directly: no intentional splitting, throttling, or partner-routing designed to disguise threshold exposure.
- A concrete plan exists for the point at which full registration becomes necessary.

**Concerning**

- Management says it is well below the threshold but does not provide exact figures on the call.
- Monitoring exists, but it is informal, legal-advisor driven, or not embedded operationally.
- Management denies any structuring issue in principle, but cannot show the policies or analysis behind that answer.

**Unacceptable**

- Management does not know current utilization against the threshold.
- No one can explain whether affiliate activity counts.
- The answer suggests the company is steering volume, legal entities, or partner flow to preserve the exemption without a clear legal framework.

**Likely failure type if poor**

- `Competence / credibility failure` if they do not know the numbers.
- `Structural thesis failure` if the business only works by skating around the threshold.

## 3. What is the real DCM path, and how much of the model actually depends on it?

**What must be shown**

- Actual status: exploratory, pre-filing, filed, or in active response.
- Written regulator feedback, open issues, and gap analysis.
- Budget, staffing, surveillance, rules, and governance buildout required to qualify.
- Quantification of how much of Year 2 and Year 3 revenue, margin, and strategic value depends on DCM approval.
- Fallback economics if DCM is delayed or denied.

**Acceptable**

- Management states the status precisely and can produce dated materials or regulator feedback.
- The remaining buildout is itemized, owned, and budgeted.
- Management quantifies how much of the forward plan requires DCM and how much does not.
- The fallback plan is not just narrative; it is economic, bounded, and internally consistent.

**Concerning**

- Management describes an active path but has limited written regulator evidence.
- The path depends on several still-unbuilt functions.
- The fallback exists conceptually, but the dependency of the model on DCM has not been quantified.

**Unacceptable**

- DCM is treated as branding rather than a regulatory dependency.
- Management cannot say what percentage of the medium-term case requires DCM.
- There is no credible path, no clear feedback loop with the CFTC, and no coherent downside plan.

**Likely failure type if poor**

- `Structural thesis failure` if DCM is central and not plausible.
- `Competence / credibility failure` if management cannot describe status accurately.

## 4. Is OCPI a benchmark, or a constructed appraisal with benchmark branding?

**What must be shown**

- Contributor count, contributor concentration, and minimum publication thresholds.
- A clear breakdown of raw executable transaction prints versus normalized, interpolated, or modeled observations.
- Fallback logic, restatement rules, methodology change control, and governance.
- A replication package sufficient for independent recalculation.
- IOSCO gap assessment and remediation plan.

**Acceptable**

- Management can quantify the composition of OCPI and does not hide the share that is modeled or normalized.
- The company can support an independent replication workstream.
- Benchmark governance is more than founder judgment, and IOSCO readiness has been assessed honestly.
- The resulting picture supports the claim that OCPI is grounded in observable market activity rather than mostly constructed estimates.

**Concerning**

- Management can describe the methodology, but the raw-print versus modeled mix is only directionally described.
- Replication is permitted in principle, but the data package is not ready.
- IOSCO is acknowledged, but the workplan is immature.

**Unacceptable**

- Management cannot quantify how much of OCPI comes from direct market evidence.
- The methodology is effectively a black box.
- Replication is resisted, or there is no credible benchmark-governance process.

**Likely failure type if poor**

- Usually `structural thesis failure`.

## 5. How is the four-layer conflict stack actually controlled?

The Ornn-specific conflict problem is not generic. The company may:

1. produce the index,
2. face customers on contracts settled to the index,
3. mark its own book using the index, and
4. trade for its own account around the same market.

**What must be shown**

- Separate governance for benchmark production.
- Independent price verification or other credible mark controls.
- Risk, hedging, collateral, default, and dispute procedures.
- Information barriers, personal trading rules, and front-running / pre-hedging controls.
- Surveillance and revenue attribution if proprietary trading exists.

**Acceptable**

- Management addresses each layer separately, not as one generic "we manage conflicts" answer.
- The marking process does not reduce to "we use our own benchmark and trust our judgment."
- Controls over employee trading and own-account activity are explicit and monitored.

**Concerning**

- Management has the right instincts, but the separation is still partly informal.
- Independent marks, barrier controls, or surveillance are still being built.
- Residual risk is described qualitatively rather than with concrete exposure metrics.

**Unacceptable**

- Management collapses benchmark production, risk management, and trading into one founder-controlled process.
- No one can explain who challenges marks, who can trade, or how customer-conflict risk is monitored.
- The answer effectively asks the investor to trust an uncontrolled circular process.

**Likely failure type if poor**

- Usually `structural thesis failure`, sometimes `competence / credibility failure`.

## 6. Is there real two-sided liquidity, and does it clear the investor's threshold framework?

This question should not be graded ad hoc. Before the management call, the investor should define what it considers minimally acceptable on:

- monthly notional,
- unique active counterparties,
- repeat-trade rate,
- completed settlements or margin cycles,
- concentration by top counterparties,
- and, where relevant, quote depth or market-maker support.

**What must be shown**

- Actual KPI history on those dimensions.
- Breakout by counterparty type and product.
- Churn, failed sales, and declined counterparties, not just success cases.

**Acceptable**

- Management provides the full KPI set and the numbers clear the investor's predeclared thresholds on most relevant dimensions.
- Activity is not entirely explained by one partner, one customer, or one temporary launch event.
- Repeat participation and completed settlement cycles support the claim that a market is forming.

**Concerning**

- There is some real activity, but it is thin, recent, or highly concentrated.
- Management has topline notional or logo evidence but not enough depth to prove durable liquidity.
- The investor has not set thresholds in advance, making the conclusion softer than it should be.

**Unacceptable**

- Liquidity proof is reduced to announcements, listings, access counts, or anecdotal enthusiasm.
- Management cannot show repeat participation, settlement completion, or meaningful two-sided flow.
- The market-formation claim depends mainly on narrative rather than observed trading behavior.

**Likely failure type if poor**

- Usually `structural thesis failure`.

## 7. Is the calculation, control, and audit-trail stack credible?

This question covers system integrity, not privacy.

**What must be shown**

- Data lineage from source input to benchmark output to contract settlement.
- Access controls over methodology changes, overrides, and production publishing.
- Immutable or otherwise reliable audit logging.
- Incident history, recovery targets, and operational resilience.
- Single-point-of-failure analysis.

**Acceptable**

- Management can trace who can change what, how changes are logged, and how outputs are reconciled.
- The stack is documented well enough that it is not purely trapped in one engineer's head.
- Recovery and operational-control evidence exists, even if the team is still lean.

**Concerning**

- The architecture is coherent, but documentation, testing, or resilience evidence is still light.
- Some controls exist in practice more than in formal process.
- One or two people remain clear operational bottlenecks.

**Unacceptable**

- No one can show reliable data lineage or who has the ability to alter production outputs.
- Auditability is informal or incomplete.
- Operational resilience depends almost entirely on one technical founder.

**Likely failure type if poor**

- Usually `remediable build gap`, but can become `competence / credibility failure` if basic lineage is unknown.

## 8. Is privacy and data-governance compliance actually understood?

This question is separate from system reliability.

**What must be shown**

- What data the company receives, stores, transfers, and exposes.
- Whether personal data, customer-confidential data, or non-US data is in scope.
- GDPR, CCPA, and cross-border transfer analysis, if relevant.
- Retention, deletion, and contractual data-use terms.

**Acceptable**

- Management can say precisely whether privacy regulation is materially in scope and why.
- The company has completed at least a defensible scoping analysis and can produce the relevant agreements or policies.
- Cross-border and contributor-data issues have been considered, not ignored.

**Concerning**

- Management has a plausible answer, but the legal or operational review is still incomplete.
- Policies exist only partially, or only because a vendor or counsel requested them.

**Unacceptable**

- Management hand-waves privacy away without analyzing customer, contributor, or geographic exposure.
- No one can say what data is subject to what restrictions.

**Likely failure type if poor**

- Usually `remediable build gap`.

## 9. Can the team govern itself through the next stage, including founder disagreement?

This question covers more than hiring. It includes decision rights and founder governance.

**What must be shown**

- Key-person map and succession plan.
- Missing control-function hires and timing.
- Founder vesting and incentive alignment.
- Decision-rights map, deadlock resolution, and board mechanics if founders disagree.

**Acceptable**

- Management is candid about key-person concentration and has a concrete hiring and succession plan.
- The company can explain who decides what, how disputes are resolved, and what happens if a founder departs or disengages.
- Incentives do not reward growth at the expense of control integrity.

**Concerning**

- The team understands its staffing gap, but governance and succession are still informal.
- Founder dynamics appear stable, but the formal deadlock mechanics are thin or not yet tested.

**Unacceptable**

- Management treats a four-person control bench as normal and not a material risk.
- No one can explain who breaks a deadlock or how control continues through a founder dispute.
- The company appears to depend on personal alignment rather than governance.

**Likely failure type if poor**

- Usually `competence / credibility failure`.

## 10. Is the tax and entity structure defensible?

This question is separate from IP.

**What must be shown**

- Why each entity exists and what each one does.
- Which entity books which revenue stream.
- Intercompany flows, transfer pricing, and state / local nexus analysis.
- Tax treatment analysis for swaps, data licensing, and other revenue types.

**Acceptable**

- Management can explain the structure coherently, and counsel or accounting work has tested it.
- Revenue mapping, intercompany economics, and tax treatment are documented or well advanced.

**Concerning**

- The structure is broadly understandable, but some analysis is still incomplete.
- Tax work exists at a high level only and may not reflect how the business is evolving.

**Unacceptable**

- Management cannot explain the point of the multi-entity structure.
- Revenue and intercompany flows do not line up with entity purpose.
- Tax analysis has effectively not been done despite the business model.

**Likely failure type if poor**

- Usually `remediable build gap`, unless the structure itself appears misleading or incoherent.

## 11. Are IP ownership, data rights, and OSS exposure actually clean?

This question is separate from tax and entity design.

**What must be shown**

- Founder, employee, and contractor assignment agreements.
- Ownership of methodology, code, and benchmark-related IP.
- Third-party data rights and contributor agreements.
- Open-source dependency review and license compatibility analysis.

**Acceptable**

- Management can show clean chain of title for core IP and data rights.
- OSS and contractor risk have been reviewed, not assumed away.
- The company has at least a defensible view of what is protectable and what is merely hard to replicate.

**Concerning**

- IP ownership is probably sound, but some assignments, audits, or data-rights reviews are incomplete.
- Protectability claims are stronger than the supporting analysis.

**Unacceptable**

- Core IP ownership is unclear.
- Data rights are ambiguous or inconsistent with how the product is described.
- OSS or contractor exposure has not been reviewed.

**Likely failure type if poor**

- Usually `remediable build gap`, but it can become `structural thesis failure` if the moat depends on rights the company does not clearly control.

## 12. What is the actual moat against incumbents and hyperscalers?

This question should force explicit discussion of Bloomberg, ICE, CME, exchanges, and major cloud or data platforms.

**What must be shown**

- Why a better-capitalized incumbent cannot reproduce the benchmark, distribution, or market structure quickly.
- Sources of defensibility: proprietary data access, regulatory positioning, workflow lock-in, switching costs, settlement network effects, or contractual exclusivity.
- Evidence, not just strategic aspiration.

**Acceptable**

- Management can articulate a concrete moat with at least one defendable non-narrative component.
- The answer includes timing and replication difficulty, not just category enthusiasm.
- The company is honest about where it has an edge and where it does not.

**Concerning**

- There may be some first-mover or relationship advantage, but it is still fragile or easily copied.
- The moat depends mainly on speed and storytelling rather than durable structural advantage.

**Unacceptable**

- Management cannot explain why a larger incumbent could not replicate the business quickly.
- The real answer is effectively "nothing prevents it."

**Likely failure type if poor**

- Usually `structural thesis failure`.

## 13. How concentrated is the business in key partners and external channels?

This question should cover dependency on distribution, listing, data, and validation partners.

**What must be shown**

- Revenue, volume, data-supply, or visibility concentration by partner.
- Contract duration, termination rights, notice periods, and exclusivity terms.
- Operational fallback if one major partner exits.

**Acceptable**

- Management knows which relationships are mission critical and can quantify the exposure.
- The contracts and operating plan show at least some mitigation for the loss of a major partner.
- Dependency is discussed directly rather than buried inside growth narrative.

**Concerning**

- Concentration is visible, but mitigation is thin or mostly prospective.
- The business remains dependent on a small set of external channels or suppliers.

**Unacceptable**

- Management cannot quantify exposure to any major partner.
- Loss of a single partner would materially impair the business, and no mitigation exists.
- The diligence burden is shifted onto investors to discover the concentration risk themselves.

**Likely failure type if poor**

- Usually `structural thesis failure`.

## 14. What should the investor conclude from the full pattern of answers?

**Acceptable overall pattern**

- Management answers directly, uses exact labels, admits what is not yet built, and provides supporting materials quickly.
- Representation and proof generally line up.
- Weak areas are framed as bounded build gaps with owners, dates, and budgets.

**Concerning overall pattern**

- Management may be directionally credible, but several claims still depend on future buildout or follow-up evidence.
- The company may be investable only with a tighter gating structure, lower valuation tolerance, or staged capital plan.

**Unacceptable overall pattern**

- Management defaults to category rhetoric, partner logos, or future-state narrative when asked for current operating facts.
- Different team members describe the same issue differently.
- Core issues remain unsupported, circular, or strategically evasive.

## Decision framework

Do not treat all unacceptable answers as equal.

### Default `pause or pass`

Use this when the issue reflects a `competence / credibility failure` or a `structural thesis failure`, especially in:

- what is live today,
- de minimis posture,
- DCM dependency,
- benchmark construction,
- conflict and mark independence,
- real liquidity,
- founder governance,
- moat,
- or partner concentration.

### Continue only with strict gating

Use this when the issue is a `remediable build gap`, for example:

- privacy work not fully completed,
- tax analysis incomplete but coherent,
- IP diligence partially assembled,
- operational controls still being formalized.

In those cases, require owners, deadlines, outside counsel or audit support, and documentary proof before closing.

### Hold for evidence, not for storytelling

Use this when management may be right, but the answer is still only a `proof gap`.

- Do not upgrade the rating based on confidence or polish.
- Time-box the dataroom request.
- If evidence does not arrive quickly, downgrade the answer.
