## Investment committee memo — Silicon Data Inc. / Compute Exchange Inc.

**To:** Investment Committee
**From:** [Analyst]
**Date:** April 7, 2026
**Re:** **Silicon Data / Compute Exchange — preliminary IC memo (public-information based)**

### Recommendation

**Authorize gated phase-two diligence only; do not approve an investment or term sheet yet.** The public record shows a credible founding team, differentiated distribution (Bloomberg and Refinitiv), meaningful third-party press coverage, and a strategic backer profile (DRW, Jump Trading Group) that lends commercial plausibility to the thesis. However, the evidence base remains overwhelmingly company-generated, and four structural issues are unresolved: **(1) entity structure, regulatory ambiguity, and the gap between the marketplace/matching positioning and forward-contract/derivatives language, (2) index methodology governance, IOSCO readiness, and benchmark independence, (3) conflicts arising from proprietary-trading-firm parentage, and (4) market formation, competitive durability, and runway.** Until management produces auditable answers on those fronts, no investment decision is supportable.

### Company snapshot

Silicon Data Inc. is building a **GPU pricing, index, and analytics platform** targeting traders, financial institutions, and compute buyers. The company launched in May 2025 alongside a **$4.7 million seed round** co-led by **DRW** and **Jump Trading Group**, with participation from Woodside AI, Wintermute Ventures, Sancus Ventures, SoGal Ventures, and several angel investors [1]. The currently persisted public-source pack does not establish total capital raised beyond that announced seed round, so later financing should be treated as unresolved until management clarifies it.

The company's CEO and founder is **Carmen Li**, described by IEEE Spectrum as an "ex-Bloomberg data wrangler" [3]. Don Wilson, founder and CEO of DRW, is a **co-founder of the affiliated Compute Exchange** entity and a lead investor in Silicon Data [4][5]. Carmen Li was **appointed CEO of Compute Exchange** in October 2025, replacing founding CEO Simeon Bochev and CTO Bjoern Metzdorf, who moved to advisory roles [6]. Li now leads both entities under what she describes as a "unified vision," drawing an explicit analogy to **LSEG's integration of Refinitiv and FTSE Russell** [6].

The product suite spans five named offerings: **SiliconNavigator** (market intelligence), **SiliconMark** (GPU benchmarking), **SiliconPriceIQ** (pricing forecasts), **SiliconCarbon** (carbon footprint tracking), and the **Silicon Index** family — daily GPU rental price indices for the H100, A100, and B200 [7][8]. The H100 index is distributed on the **Bloomberg Terminal** (ticker: SDH100RT) and, according to Silicon Data's October 8, 2025 release, on **Refinitiv** via a partnership with dxFeed; A100 (SDA100RT) and B200 (SDB200RT) tickers are also live on Bloomberg [9]. The homepage claims Silicon Data is "the only compute data provider available on Bloomberg and Refinitiv" [7]. That claim is **self-reported and unverified**.

Compute Exchange Inc. ("CX") operates a **GPU compute marketplace** — spot and reserved capacity, forward contracts, an auction mechanism, and a provider listing service [4]. CX's stated commission is **4% of transaction value** [11]. CX explicitly positions itself as a **matching platform, not a counterparty**: "TCEX facilitates matching between buyers and sellers — we don't take ownership of hardware or act as a counterparty" [12].

### What appears real today

There is enough public evidence to treat Silicon Data / CX as more than a concept, though the bar for "investable" is materially higher than the bar for "real."

**Distribution is genuine.** The Bloomberg tickers and the Refinitiv expansion are central to Silicon Data's public commercial positioning [8][9]. Bloomberg distribution improves discoverability; it should not be read as methodology endorsement by Bloomberg or Refinitiv.

**Third-party press coverage is meaningful.** IEEE Spectrum covered the H100 index launch in May 2025 [3]. WatersTechnology — a leading financial technology trade publication — covered the GPU index story in October 2025, noting DRW backing and the Bloomberg/Refinitiv distribution [13]. AIthority separately covered the Compute Exchange leadership transition and the stated integrated strategy across the two entities [6]. None of this coverage constitutes an independent audit of methodology or governance.

**The data surface appears live.** The company claims over **3.5 million data points** (self-reported) and **150,000 daily verified pricing records** across **40-50 countries** and **50-100 platforms** [7][15]. A data portal (portal.silicondata.com) and API documentation site (docs.silicondata.com) are publicly visible [7]. Those claims remain **self-reported and unaudited**.

**The backer profile is commercially relevant.** DRW and Jump Trading Group are major proprietary trading firms with deep derivative-market expertise and infrastructure. Don Wilson's personal involvement as a CX co-founder signals conviction beyond a passive financial investment [5]. Wintermute Ventures adds crypto/digital-asset market-making adjacency. This backer set is well-matched to a compute-commoditization thesis, though it also introduces the conflict questions discussed below.

### Competitive context

Silicon Data is not operating in a vacuum. Its own public materials target financial institutions building swaps, futures, and structured notes tied to compute benchmarks [7], while Compute Exchange separately pushes a marketplace and forward-contract workflow [4][12]. That means Silicon Data is competing simultaneously for benchmark adoption, data distribution, and market-structure relevance. The competitive picture is already crowded enough that any thesis claiming Silicon Data's indices will become the singular standard is premature.

Silicon Data's comparative advantages are: (a) early Bloomberg/Refinitiv distribution, (b) a backer set (DRW/Jump) with deep derivatives-market infrastructure, and (c) the CX marketplace as a potential proprietary data moat. The comparative weaknesses are: (a) the same early-stage organizational thinness common to many entrants, (b) methodology opacity, and (c) regulatory positioning that has not been tested. The durable winner in GPU indexing is more likely to be determined by methodology credibility and institutional adoption than by first-mover timing alone.

### Team and operating-model risk

The public-facing team is thin. **Carmen Li** (CEO of both entities) is the only publicly named senior leader at Silicon Data itself [1][3]. **Don Wilson** and **Suna Said** (CEO of Woodside AI, early investor in Palantir, Instacart, Anduril, and others) are co-founders of CX but in governance rather than operating roles [4]. Blog authorship credits include **Yang Gao**, **Yuhua Yu**, **Platon Slynko**, and **Jason Cornick**, but their titles and seniority are not publicly disclosed [15]. Total headcount is not disclosed.

The public record does **not** show dedicated legal, compliance, risk, treasury, or surveillance hires at either entity. That is a **public-record gap, not proof those functions do not exist**, but it is material because: (a) the company is positioning for derivatives and financial-product use cases, (b) the dual-CEO structure across two entities demands clear governance, and (c) the departure of prior CX leadership (Bochev and Metzdorf) already demonstrates leadership-transition risk [6].

Carmen Li's financial services background is confirmed by public reporting [3], but the public record does not detail her specific roles or tenure at Bloomberg beyond the "data wrangler" characterization. Phase-two diligence should request a detailed CV and reference calls.

### Core risk #1: entity structure, regulatory ambiguity, and forward-contract/derivatives positioning

Three named entities appear in public materials: **Silicon Data Inc.** (the index/analytics company), **Silicon Derivatives Inc.** (named in Silicon Data's terms of use — entity name discrepancy unexplained), and **Compute Exchange Inc.** / "CEX" / "TCEX" (the marketplace) [18][19]. The relationship between "Silicon Data Inc." and "Silicon Derivatives Inc." is not publicly explained. The terms of use are attributed to "Silicon Derivatives Inc." while the copyright and trademark belong to "Silicon Data Inc." [18]. That discrepancy may be benign (name change, parent/subsidiary) or may indicate a more complex entity structure designed to house different regulatory risks. Until clarified, it is a flag.

CX's marketplace terms explicitly state: "CEX does not provide services subject to a regulatory license" [19]. CX's forward-contract page describes a matching service — buyers specify needs, CX matches with supply partners, and "once matched, buyers and sellers negotiate and execute terms directly" [12]. That positioning is consistent with a marketplace/platform model that may not require CFTC registration. However, the forward contracts involve **locking in capacity at a fixed price for future dates** [12], which at minimum raises the question of whether any such contracts could be characterized as **forwards, swaps, or futures** under CFTC jurisdiction, particularly if they become standardized or financially settled.

That question sharpens because Silicon Data's own homepage tells financial institutions to use SiliconIndex as the benchmark to build **swaps, futures, and structured notes** tied to GPU markets [7]. If that roadmap is real, the current "no regulatory license" posture is temporary at best. Phase-two diligence must map each product, each entity, and the regulatory pathway for each — including whether the forward contracts as currently structured are lawfully exempt from CFTC oversight, and what the plan is for exchange-traded derivatives.

### Core risk #2: index methodology governance, IOSCO readiness, and benchmark independence

The Silicon Index is the fulcrum of the entire thesis: if the index is credible, the downstream products (swaps, futures, structured notes) have a foundation; if it is not, the entire value chain is fragile.

The company's methodology blog describes broad principles — **150,000 daily records**, **40-50 countries**, **50-100 platforms**, **proprietary normalization** that adjusts for lease type, provider, location, and hardware specifications [15]. The index product page adds claims of **80%+ coverage** of the global H100 rental market, "statistically robust" outlier removal, and "independent oversight" by unnamed "calculation agents and data validators" [8]. These are reasonable design principles for a commodity index.

However, the public record does **not** show:

- A **published methodology document** (the blog post describes principles, not the actual methodology) [15]
- Any **IOSCO Principles for Financial Benchmarks** mapping or self-assessment [8][15]
- The identity or independence of the claimed "**calculation agents and data validators**" [8]
- **Contributor concentration** (what share of the 150,000 daily records comes from any single platform)
- **Minimum publication thresholds** (what happens if data sources fall below a threshold)
- **Restatement history** or error-correction procedures
- The percentage of data that is **direct transaction data** versus **normalized/estimated**
- Any **independent audit** or **replication package**
- Any **governance structure** for methodology changes (who approves changes, what notice is given to users)

The normalization framework is described as "proprietary" [8][15], which is standard for commercial index providers but creates opacity risk when the same organization is also positioning for exchange-traded derivatives on those indices. If Silicon Data both produces the benchmark and is affiliated with the marketplace where products referencing that benchmark trade, the independence question is structurally identical to the one IOSCO's principles are designed to address.

### Core risk #3: conflicts from proprietary-trading-firm parentage

DRW and Jump Trading Group are among the world's largest proprietary trading firms, active across equities, fixed income, commodities, and crypto [5][13]. Their co-investment in Silicon Data is commercially logical — if GPU compute becomes a traded commodity, DRW and Jump would be natural participants — but it also creates a **structural conflict** that requires explicit governance:

- **Information asymmetry.** If DRW or Jump trade in compute markets (spot, forward, or derivatives), and Silicon Data produces the benchmark used to price those trades, the index producer's investors may have informational advantages. Across the reviewed public materials, there is no disclosed information-barrier, Chinese-wall, or governance-committee structure between Silicon Data and its investor-backers [4][5][7][8][12][19].
- **Index influence.** As lead investors, DRW and Jump likely have board seats or observer rights. The public record does not show whether they have any influence over methodology decisions, publication timing, or data-source selection.
- **Marketplace participation.** If DRW or Jump participate in CX's marketplace as buyers, sellers, or auction participants, the co-founder relationship (Don Wilson is a CX co-founder) creates the appearance — and potentially the reality — of preferential access. The public record is silent on this.
- **Self-dealing in derivatives.** Silicon Data's own public positioning of the index as infrastructure for swaps, futures, and structured notes [7] raises the question of whether DRW and Jump would be among the first liquidity providers for such products. If so, the backer set simultaneously invests in the index company, co-founds the marketplace, and provides liquidity for derivatives referencing the index. That is not inherently disqualifying — similar structures exist in traditional commodity markets — but it requires transparent governance, surveillance, and independent oversight that the public record does not currently evidence.

The Silicon Data index product page describes the index as "Independent and Objective" and notes it is "Backed by financial institutions including DRW and Jump Trading" [8]. Listing the prop-trading backers in the same sentence as an independence claim, without disclosing governance controls, is a **presentation risk** at minimum.

### Core risk #4: market formation, competitive durability, and runway

Even if the legal, governance, and conflict issues are resolvable, the harder question is whether GPU compute derivatives will form a liquid market before Silicon Data's capital runs out.

**Runway.** The disclosed funding in the persisted public-source pack is **$4.7 million** from May 2025 [1]. The company operates two entities (Silicon Data and CX), has built at least five named products, and maintains Bloomberg/Refinitiv distribution. Burn rate is not disclosed, but seed-stage capital supporting this breadth of activity is likely to require follow-on funding within 12-18 months of the seed close. That window may already be approaching. Cash, burn, and runway are **P0 issues** for phase-two diligence.

**Market formation.** The harder question is whether GPU-compute financial products can form repeatable liquidity before Silicon Data's capital runs out. The marketplace model (CX) provides a potential advantage over pure-index plays because it generates proprietary transaction data, but the public materials reviewed still show **no completed-trade metrics, volumes, or active counterparty counts** [4][11][12][19].

**Competitive durability.** The index business is defensible only if (a) the methodology is credibly superior, (b) institutional adoption creates switching costs, or (c) the index is embedded in enough derivative contracts to create lock-in. None of those conditions is yet established. The "first mover on Bloomberg/Refinitiv" claim [7] is valuable but not a moat if a competitor publishes a methodology that institutional users find more credible, more transparent, or IOSCO-aligned.

### Bottom line

Silicon Data / Compute Exchange is a credible early-stage entrant in GPU compute market infrastructure. The team has relevant financial-services experience, the backer profile is commercially aligned, the distribution footprint (Bloomberg, Refinitiv) is differentiated, and the dual-entity structure (data + marketplace) has strategic logic. Third-party coverage from IEEE Spectrum, WatersTechnology, and AIthority is meaningful independent validation of the thesis, if not of the product [3][13][6].

But the evidence is still too thin, too self-referential, and too structurally incomplete to support an investment decision. The entity structure is opaque. The regulatory posture is explicitly disclaimed ("CEX does not provide services subject to a regulatory license" [19]) even as the company positions its benchmark for swaps, futures, and structured notes [7]. The methodology is proprietary and unaudited. The conflicts from prop-trading parentage are unaddressed by any visible governance structure. And the public record shows no evidence of actual trading activity, customer revenue, or marketplace volumes.

The right next step is **gated phase-two diligence** focused on: (a) a clean entity-by-entity, product-by-product legal and regulatory map; (b) the full index methodology and an independent replication workstream; (c) information-barrier and governance documentation addressing DRW/Jump conflicts; (d) financial statements, burn rate, and runway; (e) evidence of marketplace traction (trade count, volume, counterparty count); and (f) the plan and timeline for any CFTC-regulated products. If management can resolve those issues credibly, Silicon Data could be a differentiated infrastructure investment in a market with genuine structural tailwinds. If not, the likely conclusion should be **pass due to regulatory ambiguity, methodology opacity, unaddressed conflicts, and organizational thinness**. Valuation terms are not publicly disclosed beyond the seed financing, so there is no basis for a risk-adjusted entry-price view.
