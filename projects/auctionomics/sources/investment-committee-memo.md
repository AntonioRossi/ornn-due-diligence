## Investment committee memo — Auctionomics Inc. / OCX Group Inc. (OneChronos)

**To:** Investment Committee
**From:** [Analyst]
**Date:** April 9, 2026
**Re:** **Auctionomics / OneChronos — preliminary IC memo (public-information based)**

### Recommendation

**Authorize gated phase-two diligence only; do not approve an investment or term sheet yet.** The public record shows a credible partnership between a Nobel-laureate-led market-design firm and a well-capitalized, SEC/FINRA-regulated trading venue operator. The thesis — that GPU compute can be made tradable through combinatorial auctions — is intellectually serious and addresses a real market gap. However, the evidence base is still overwhelmingly announcement-driven rather than execution-proven, and five structural issues remain unresolved: **(1) partnership legal form, governance, and durability, (2) product regulatory pathway for compute forwards and futures, (3) market-design feasibility — whether combinatorial auctions can create durable liquidity in compute, (4) absence of any visible compute-market traction or participants, and (5) the gap between OneChronos' equities expertise and compute-market operational requirements.** Until the partnership can demonstrate a working product, named participants, and a credible regulatory pathway, the compute-market initiative remains a high-quality announcement without operating proof.

### Company snapshot

**Auctionomics Inc.** is a market design and auction consulting firm based in **Palo Alto, California**, co-founded in **2008** by **Paul Milgrom** — a Stanford professor and co-recipient of the **2020 Nobel Prize in Economics** for auction theory — and **Dr. Silvia Console Battilana**, who serves as CEO and holds a PhD in Economics from Stanford [1][2]. The firm's most prominent achievement is the design of the **FCC Broadcast Incentive Auction**, which reallocated radio spectrum to enable 5G and television streaming and earned the team an **Emmy Award** [1]. Auctionomics describes itself as specializing in "under-the-hood complexity that appears simple to users" for high-stakes auction environments [1]. The public team page lists **25+ members**, though most appear to be academic advisors and consultants from institutions including Stanford, Princeton, MIT, UBC, and TU Munich rather than full-time operating staff [3]. No external funding has been publicly disclosed; the firm appears to operate as a self-funded consulting and software business.

**OCX Group Inc.** is the parent company of **OneChronos**, a technology company headquartered in **SoHo, New York City**, operating at the intersection of capital markets, mechanism design, and operations research [4][5]. OneChronos was co-founded by **Kelly Littlepage** (CEO) and **Stephen Johnson**, went through **Y Combinator in 2016**, and has raised **$80 million or more** from investors including **Addition**, **BoxGroup**, **DCVC**, **DST Global**, **Green Visor Capital**, and **9Yards Capital** [6][7]. The most recent disclosed round was **$32 million in expansion capital** led by Addition in **November 2024** [7]. In **September 2025**, **Douglas L. Peterson** — former CEO of **S&P Global** (who grew its market cap from $16 billion to over $150 billion) and former COO of Citibank — was appointed **Executive Chairman** [8]. Milgrom serves on OneChronos' **scientific advisory board** [6].

OneChronos operates through its subsidiary **OneChronos Markets LLC**, a wholly-owned **broker-dealer** that runs an **alternative trading system (ATS)** for U.S. equities, **regulated by the SEC and FINRA** (CRD# 286322, Member FINRA/SIPC) [5][9]. The ATS uses **Smart Market technology** — combinatorial auctions that use mathematical optimization to match counterparties based on complex, multi-asset preferences [1][7]. By **September 2025**, the ATS had reached **$10 billion in daily trading volume**, entered **FINRA's top-10 ATSs** for the first time, and facilitated over **$2 trillion** in securities transactions since its 2022 launch [8]. The company also launched a **spot FX trading venue** in **March 2026** and is expanding into **Europe and Asia** [8][10].

On **July 29, 2025**, the two firms announced a partnership to develop the **first financial market for GPU and compute capacity** [1]. The market will use OneChronos' Smart Market technology to deliver **real-time price discovery and liquidity through bilateral forwards**, enabling participants to lock in future compute capacity and pricing [1]. Target participants include investors in data centers, data center operators, power generators, chip manufacturers, and enterprise AI buyers [1].

### What appears real today

There is enough public evidence to treat this partnership as more than a concept, though the bar for "investable" is materially higher than the bar for "real."

**The equities platform is proven at scale.** OneChronos' ATS has operated since 2022, reached $10 billion in daily volume, entered FINRA's top-10 ATSs, and facilitated $2 trillion in transactions [7][8]. SEC Rule 605 execution quality reports are publicly available going back to June 2022 [9]. This is not a paper product — the underlying technology works in a demanding, regulated environment.

**The team and governance are credible.** Milgrom's auction-theory credentials are independently verified by the Nobel Prize, and Auctionomics' track record in complex auction design (FCC Incentive Auction, spectrum auctions across multiple countries) is documented by third parties [1][2][6]. Peterson's appointment as Executive Chairman brings institutional credibility and global-markets expertise [8]. Littlepage and Johnson bring operating experience from Goldman Sachs, Accenture, and quantitative asset management [5].

**Third-party coverage is meaningful.** Upstarts Media (Alex Konrad, formerly Forbes) published an in-depth feature on the partnership [6]. TechSpot covered the announcement independently [11]. The partnership was distributed via BusinessWire and picked up by Yahoo Finance [1]. This is not self-published press alone.

**The backers are well-matched.** $80 million or more in venture capital from institutional fintech investors, plus a $32 million expansion round in November 2024, suggests financial capacity to execute [6][7]. However, the compute-market initiative's specific capital allocation is not disclosed.

**What is still missing:** The public record does **not** show any named compute-market participants, any completed compute-market transactions, any regulatory approval or exemption analysis for compute forwards, any published product specification, pricing mechanism, or standardization framework, or any timeline for the compute market to begin operating. Littlepage stated in the Upstarts article that there were no specific businesses signed on as of July 2025 [6]. The product remains in development.

### Competitive context

The GPU-compute market-infrastructure space is now contested by at least three visible entrants with different approaches:

**Ornn** is pursuing a **benchmark-plus-bilateral-swaps** model. The **Ornn Compute Price Index (OCPI)** was added to the Bloomberg Terminal in April 2026, and the company has announced partnerships with Hydra Host (30,000+ GPUs under management), Architect (pending exchange-traded futures), and Kalshi (event contracts). Ornn operates as a **direct counterparty** for cash-settled swaps under the CFTC's de minimis threshold, with a stated DCM application path [12].

**Silicon Data / Compute Exchange** is pursuing an **index-plus-marketplace** model. Silicon Data's H100 rental index is distributed on Bloomberg and Refinitiv, and Compute Exchange operates a matching platform for spot, reserved, and forward compute capacity. DRW and Jump Trading Group co-led the seed round [13].

**Auctionomics / OneChronos** is pursuing an **auction-based market-design** model — combinatorial auctions that optimize across multiple dimensions (GPU type, power, storage, location, time) to find efficient multi-party matches. This is structurally different from both Ornn's bilateral-swap approach and Silicon Data's one-to-one matching model.

The competitive question is not which model is theoretically superior but which can **form durable liquidity first**. Ornn has live bilateral activity, Bloomberg distribution, and partner integrations. Silicon Data has an index, terminal distribution, and a marketplace product surface. Auctionomics/OneChronos has a proven technology platform (in equities) and the strongest market-design credentials, but **no visible compute-market product or participants yet**. The auction-based approach could be structurally more efficient for heterogeneous compute resources, but it also requires more participants to achieve critical mass than a bilateral model.

### Team and operating-model risk

The partnership brings together two organizations with different operating models and cultures. **Auctionomics** is primarily an academic consulting firm — the 25+ team members listed are overwhelmingly professors, PhD students, and researchers [3]. **OneChronos** is an operating technology company with demonstrated ability to run regulated infrastructure at scale [7][8]. The compute-market initiative sits at the intersection.

Key personnel questions remain:

- **Milgrom's role**: He is on OneChronos' scientific advisory board and is a co-founder of Auctionomics, but his day-to-day involvement in the compute-market initiative is not publicly disclosed [2][6]. He is a Stanford professor, suggesting this is not his full-time activity.
- **Console Battilana's role**: She runs Auctionomics' day-to-day operations and was quoted in the partnership announcement, but the team's operating capacity beyond auction consulting has not been publicly demonstrated [1][2].
- **Dedicated compute-market team**: The public record does not show a dedicated team for the compute-market initiative, dedicated legal or compliance staff, or control-function hires specific to this product.

The operating-model risk is concentrated in the **gap between market design and market operation**. Designing a combinatorial auction for compute is an intellectual challenge well-suited to Auctionomics' team. Operating an institutional marketplace — with custody, settlement, risk management, surveillance, regulatory compliance, and 24/7 operations — is a different kind of challenge. OneChronos has demonstrated the latter capability for equities but not for compute.

### Core risk #1: partnership structure, governance, and durability

The partnership between Auctionomics and OCX Group Inc. is described in the press release as a "partnership" without specifying the legal form — joint venture, contractual arrangement, equity investment, or informal collaboration [1]. The public record does not show:

- Whether the partnership involves any equity exchange, revenue sharing, or IP licensing
- How governance decisions are made (who controls product design, market rules, regulatory strategy, and participant access)
- How the partnership can be terminated and what happens to the product if it is
- Whether there is a dedicated legal entity for the compute-market initiative
- How conflicts are managed between Auctionomics' consulting client interests and the compute-market initiative

The durability question is material. If this partnership is informal or purely contractual, it can be dissolved easily. If Milgrom or Console Battilana redirects attention to other consulting engagements, or if OneChronos prioritizes FX and equities expansion over the compute market, the initiative could stall without formal governance enforcing continuation.

### Core risk #2: product regulatory pathway

The compute-market product is described as operating through an **off-exchange marketplace** using **bilateral forwards** [1]. The press release describes it as enabling participants to "lock in future capacity and pricing in advance" [1]. Several regulatory questions are unresolved:

- **Forward vs. futures/swaps classification**: bilateral forwards for future delivery of compute could be exempt from CFTC jurisdiction as commercial forwards, or they could be classified as swaps or futures depending on standardization, financial settlement, and the role of the platform in price discovery. The public record does not show any outside-counsel analysis or regulatory determination.
- **ATS scope**: OneChronos Markets LLC's ATS registration is for U.S. equities [9]. The compute market is described as "off-exchange" [1], suggesting it would operate separately from the ATS. The regulatory basis for this separate operation is not disclosed.
- **Broker-dealer requirements**: If the compute-forward product is classified as a security or swap, broker-dealer or swap-dealer registration may be required. The current regulatory footprint does not cover this.
- **No disclosed regulatory interaction**: Unlike Ornn (which has a stated DCM application path), the Auctionomics/OneChronos partnership has not disclosed any regulatory filings, applications, or interactions for the compute-market initiative.

This is not necessarily disqualifying — the product may be early enough that formal regulatory engagement has not yet occurred — but it means that the regulatory pathway is entirely open and could materially constrain or reshape the product.

### Core risk #3: market-design feasibility for compute

The intellectual case for combinatorial auctions in compute is plausible: GPU resources are heterogeneous (type, memory, interconnect, location, power, availability window), and a system that can optimize across these dimensions simultaneously could produce better allocations than bilateral matching. Console Battilana stated that "we spent a lot of our time defining what is exactly for sale" [6][11], and Milgrom described the standardization framework as a "magic sauce" that he would not detail publicly [6].

The feasibility risks are:

- **Standardization**: The "building block" for compute trading has not been publicly described. If the standardization is too coarse, the auction misses the heterogeneity that makes compute allocation complex. If too fine, the auction becomes illiquid. The public record does not show the framework.
- **Liquidity formation**: Combinatorial auctions require multiple participants with diverse preferences to produce efficient outcomes. The equities market provides natural liquidity because of institutional trading mandates. The compute market does not have this built-in. There is no evidence of committed participants.
- **Price discovery in a thin market**: Milgrom's comparison to the wheat-market evolution is apt but also reveals the timescale problem — commodity markets took decades to develop the infrastructure and liquidity that made them institutionally useful. The question is whether this can happen faster for compute.
- **Compute-specific operational challenges**: Compute resources are perishable (unused GPU-hours expire), geographically constrained (latency, data residency), and quality-variable (networking, storage, cooling). Physical delivery adds complexity that financial-only instruments like equities do not face.

### Core risk #4: market formation, competitive timing, and traction

Even if the auction design is sound and the regulatory pathway resolves, the hardest question is whether the Auctionomics/OneChronos partnership can form a liquid compute market before competitors establish the standards.

**Timing gap**: Ornn already has live bilateral activity, partner integrations, and Bloomberg distribution. Silicon Data has a published index and a marketplace with visible product pages. Auctionomics/OneChronos has an announcement and a proven equities platform but no visible compute-market product, participants, or timeline [1][6].

**Chicken-and-egg**: The marketplace needs participants to generate liquidity, but participants need liquidity to justify joining. Johnson acknowledged this indirectly: "We think there's some interest in this from the broad ecosystem, and the more diversity of participants we have, the better and healthier the market will be" [6]. No specific participant interest has been named publicly.

**Capital allocation**: OneChronos is simultaneously expanding its equities ATS, launching FX, entering Europe and Japan, and building the compute market [8][10]. With $80 million or more in total disclosed funding, the question is how much capital, engineering, and management attention is allocated specifically to the compute initiative versus the proven equities and FX businesses.

**Structural advantage possibility**: If the compute market does require multi-dimensional optimization (GPU type × location × time × power × interconnect), then a combinatorial auction may genuinely be the better market structure. This would give Auctionomics/OneChronos a structural advantage that simpler bilateral models cannot replicate. But this advantage is theoretical until proven in practice with real participants and real volume.

### Bottom line

The Auctionomics/OneChronos partnership is the most intellectually credible approach to compute-market infrastructure among visible entrants. The combination of a Nobel-laureate market designer, a regulated and scaled trading-venue operator, and institutional-grade governance (Peterson as Executive Chairman) is distinctive. The underlying technology works at scale in equities.

But the compute-market initiative is still at the **announcement stage**. The public record shows no product specification, no named participants, no completed transactions, no regulatory pathway analysis, no partnership legal structure, and no timeline for launch. The right next step is **gated phase-two diligence** focused on: (a) the partnership legal structure, governance, and IP arrangements; (b) the product specification, standardization framework, and regulatory pathway; (c) evidence of participant interest, letters of intent, or pipeline; (d) the compute-market team, budget, and timeline versus the equities/FX expansion; and (e) the competitive positioning versus Ornn and Silicon Data, which are further along in visible execution.

If the partnership can demonstrate a working product, named institutional participants, and a credible regulatory pathway, this could become a differentiated infrastructure investment leveraging genuine market-design expertise. If the initiative remains in announcement mode while competitors establish operating standards, the likely conclusion should be **pass due to execution gap, regulatory ambiguity, and competitive timing risk**.
