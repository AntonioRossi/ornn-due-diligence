## Draft 2-page investment committee memo

**To:** Investment Committee
**From:** [Analyst]
**Date:** April 6, 2026
**Re:** **Ornn - preliminary IC memo (public-information based)**

### Recommendation

**Authorize phase-two diligence; do not approve an investment or term sheet yet.**
This is not an invest/decline memo on final terms because public information does not yet resolve the four issues that matter most: **(1) product/entity/regulatory mapping, (2) OCPI governance quality, (3) counterparty/collateral/capital adequacy, and (4) real two-sided liquidity.**

### Company snapshot

Ornn is trying to build the **pricing and risk infrastructure for AI compute**. Public materials position the company around the **Ornn Compute Price Index (OCPI)** as the reference price for compute and around **cash-settled products** for compute providers, buyers, lenders, and traders. The data portal and docs show a real API/data surface, including endpoints for daily index values, historical data, volatility, utilization ratios, and memory pricing. On **April 2, 2026**, Ornn announced that OCPI had been added to the **Bloomberg Terminal** and said that more than **400** data center operators, investors, and AI companies were already accessing the platform. ([ornn.com][1])

### Why this matters

The investment case is not "another AI app." It is a **market-structure** bet. If compute becomes an underwritten infrastructure input, closer to power, freight, or other financeable inputs, then the company that owns the benchmark, embeds the data into institutional workflows, and helps form early liquidity could create durable value. Ornn's own public case is that AI companies, data-center operators, lenders, and investors need better tools to hedge price volatility and GPU depreciation. The company announced a **$5.7 million seed round** on **October 28, 2025**, led by Crucible Ventures and Vine Ventures with participation from Link Ventures and Box Group. ([PR Newswire][2])

### What appears real today

There is enough public evidence to treat Ornn as more than a concept. First, the benchmark/data layer appears live: OCPI exists, the API exists, and Bloomberg distribution is now public. Second, Ornn has visible partner distribution: on **October 22, 2025**, Hydra announced a partnership integrating real-time infrastructure data from **30,000+ GPUs under management** into Ornn's indices and analytics. Third, on **January 21, 2026**, Architect announced that AX would imminently launch exchange-traded compute futures tied to Ornn Data LLC's indices, **pending regulatory approval**; public materials also show AX operating through **Architect Bermuda Ltd.** under Bermuda regulation. Fourth, **DatacenterDynamics** reported on **March 4, 2026** that Kalshi users could trade markets on GPU compute prices verified using Ornn data. ([PR Newswire][3])

### What is attractive

The strongest part of the story is that Ornn appears to be trying to capture **three reinforcing layers at once**: benchmark data, risk-transfer products, and liquidity/distribution. That is strategically appealing because benchmark businesses can become embedded and recurring; once a pricing convention is accepted, it can support forward curves, underwriting, and structured products. The Bloomberg milestone is especially relevant because it suggests OCPI is at least becoming visible inside institutional tooling, while the Hydra and Architect relationships suggest Ornn is attempting to source real market data and distribute products through multiple channels rather than relying only on its own venue. ([PR Newswire][3])

### Core risk #1: legal and regulatory ambiguity

This is the largest diligence issue. A current public search-result snippet for Ornn.com describes the company as **"the first U.S.-regulated derivatives exchange for GPU compute."** The homepage itself uses language like **"the benchmark, the exchange, the standard"** and **"regulated, transparent, trusted."** But Ornn's **data terms** say the services are provided solely for **informational and reference purposes** and do **not** constitute exchange services, brokerage services, or an offer to enter into any transaction. Separately, the **swap risk disclosure** says cash-settled compute futures swaps referencing OCPI are offered by **ORNNX LLC**, a **de minimis swap dealer**; it explicitly says there is **no organized exchange** for those swaps and that Ornn is the direct counterparty, not a clearinghouse or insured institution. The regulatory page lists a **rulebook**, **fee schedules**, and a **participant and clearing agreement**, but the public record still does not cleanly answer which products are live, under which entity, and in which jurisdiction. The Architect release adds a third entity, **Ornn Data LLC**, as the index provider for partner-listed contracts that were still **pending regulatory approval** on January 21, 2026. ([ornn.com][4])

### Core risk #2: counterparty, collateral, and balance-sheet risk

If Ornn is taking principal exposure, it should be underwritten more like an **OTC principal / emerging market-infrastructure business** than like a pure software or data company. Ornn's own disclosure says customers can lose more than posted collateral; there may be immediate margin calls; positions can be liquidated without prior notice; collateral may or may not be segregated depending on the CSA; Ornn may trade for its own account; and Ornn has no fiduciary duty as counterparty. These are not unusual concepts in institutional derivatives, but they become acute for an early-stage company unless backed by strong capitalization, risk limits, collateral controls, and a credible default-management framework. None of that is yet visible on the public record. ([ornn.com][5])

### Core risk #3: OCPI governance and liquidity proof

OCPI is the fulcrum of the whole model, but the public record does not yet make it institutionally underwritable. Ornn's risk disclosure says OCPI depends on **proprietary methodology**, uses **third-party provider data**, and can be changed by Ornn. The data terms say users do not receive rights to underlying data sources, weighting schemes, models, or algorithms, and should not rely on the services as the sole basis for business or investment decisions. Bloomberg distribution and the reported **400+ platform users** are encouraging, but public materials still do not disclose the metrics that really matter for this kind of company: contributor concentration, restatement history, open interest, notional traded, spreads, active counterparty count, or repeat hedging behavior. ([ornn.com][6])

### Bottom line

Ornn is one of the more interesting companies in the emerging overlap between **AI infrastructure** and **financial market infrastructure**. There is real signal here: a live benchmark, a visible API/data product, Bloomberg availability, partner integrations, and a plausible underlying pain point. But the current public evidence is **not yet enough** to approve an investment. My recommendation is to move forward only to **gated phase-two diligence** using the request list above. If management can clearly resolve the legal structure, show robust OCPI governance, demonstrate real liquidity/commercial traction, and prove that counterparty risk is tightly controlled, this could become a differentiated infrastructure investment. If they cannot, the likely conclusion should be **pass due to legal opacity and balance-sheet risk**. ([PR Newswire][3])
